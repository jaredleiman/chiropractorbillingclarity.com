AT Modifier Chiropractic Medicare Requirements: The Complete Guide for Independent DCs

AI Compliance · Medicare Chiropractic Billing

AI Chart Notes and Chiropractic Upcoding: What CERT and RAC Reviewers Are Catching

The Blue Cross Blue Shield Association's March 2026 report on AI-driven hospital upcoding quantified a problem that was already happening in chiropractic offices across the country. Chiropractic Medicare billing has the highest error rate in CERT of any specialty — and the AI SOAP-note tools now standard in DC practices are making the pattern worse, not better, unless there's a deliberate review step between the AI output and the submitted claim.

Why Chiropractic Error Rates Are Already High

Medicare covers only three chiropractic codes — 98940, 98941, 98942 — and only for manual manipulation of the spine to treat a documented subluxation. The documentation requirements are narrow and specific:

  • The precise level(s) of subluxation by spinal segment
  • The mechanism of diagnosis (PART — Pain, Asymmetry, Range of motion, Tissue tone changes)
  • An initial treatment plan with measurable objectives and a discharge date
  • The AT modifier for active/corrective treatment (not maintenance)

Historical CERT error rates for chiropractic have run above 40% — higher than any other specialty. Most errors are documentation failures, not fraud: missing PART findings, absent treatment plans, AT modifier used when the care is maintenance, or subluxation levels that don't match the adjustment documented.

How AI Notes Make the Pattern Worse

  • Subluxation level templating. AI-generated SOAP notes that auto-populate "C5-C6, T2-T3, L4-L5 subluxation" across every patient produce a statistical pattern auditors catch immediately. Real subluxation documentation varies by patient and by visit.
  • PART findings as copy-paste. AI notes that include identical PART findings visit-to-visit fail medical review. The documentation must evolve with the patient's response to care.
  • 98941 over 98940. Three-to-four-region adjustments reimburse higher. AI notes that default to three regions regardless of actual treatment drive a 98941/98940 ratio outside peer norms.
  • AT modifier on maintenance care. The bright line in Medicare chiropractic coverage. AI notes that carry forward "active treatment" language after the patient has plateaued are the single largest source of overpayment recovery in the specialty.
  • Extra-spinal manipulations (98943). Not covered by Medicare. AI notes that include extra-spinal work as part of a covered visit without the GY modifier produce clean denial patterns.

What Medicare Is Already Doing

RAC auditors have had chiropractic on their issue list for over a decade. What changed in 2025 is the AI sensitivity of the RAC claim-selection process itself. A DC with a 98941/98940 ratio of 85/15 in a region where peers run 55/45 is being pulled for record request routinely — within one billing quarter, not two years later.

The CERT error rate feeds into your MAC's statistical profile of your NPI. Two consecutive years of above-peer error rates trigger TPE placement. TPE can demand 20-40 records with short turnaround and measurable downstream effects on cash flow.

A Self-Audit: Read Five SOAP Notes Against the Four Requirements

  1. Pull five recent 98941 encounters.
  2. For each: does the subluxation level documented match the adjustment performed? Are PART findings specific to the visit, or templated?
  3. Is the AT modifier supported by an active treatment plan with a goal and an end date?
  4. If the patient has been in care for 6+ weeks, is there a re-exam note showing continued functional improvement?
  5. Any extra-spinal work in the chart — is it coded correctly (non-covered 98943, not hidden inside the covered code)?

The Bottom Line

Chiropractic was already the most-audited Medicare specialty on a percentage basis before AI SOAP notes arrived. The BCBS findings make it clear that AI-assisted documentation without a clinician review step doesn't reduce error rates — it industrializes them. The practices that build explicit subluxation-level and PART-finding review into their note-signing workflow will be fine. The ones that don't will end up on every RAC issue list for the next five years.
